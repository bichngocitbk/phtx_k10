Question Answer (click to reveal)
1. Where was the Exalted One journeying when he first met the Brahmin Dona?
He was journeying along the highroad in Kosala.
2. What did the Brahmin see on the Lord's footprints?
He saw the wheelmarks with their thousand spokes, with their rims and hubs and all their complete attributes.
3. Why was he amazed at the sight of the footprints?
He was amazed because he recognised them as the second of the 32 marks of a Great Being for whom two ways would open: He would become either a wheel-turning King or a Buddha.
4. What did the Lord do next?
He stepped aside from the highroad and sat down at the root of a tree, sitting cross-legged, holding his body upright and setting mindfulness in front of him.
5. What did the Brahmin do then?
After following up the Lord's footprints, he saw the Lord seated there.
6. What was the Buddha like then?
He was comely, faith-inspiring, with calmed senses and tranquil mind, in the attainment of composure by masterly control.
7. What simile is used to describe the Buddha then?
The simile of an elephant is used: it is tamed, alert, and perfectly trained.
8. What did the Brahmin ask the Lord?
He asked the Lord whether he would be a Deva, a Gandhabba, a Yakkha, or a human being.
9. What did the Lord answer?
He said that he would be none of them.
10. Did the Brahmin understand what the Lord said?
No, he didn't. He didn't understand what the Lord said.
11. What did the Lord explain about his answer?
He explained that he would become a Deva, a Gandhabba, a Yakkha, or a human being if those defilements (asavas) were not destroyed in him.
12. What simile did the Lord use to describe the asavas totally destroyed in him?
He used the simile of a palm tree whose crown had been cut off so that it couldn't grow again.
13. What simile did the Lord use to describe a Buddha in the world?
He used the simile of a lotus that stands rising out of the water, unsoiled by the water though born in the water, grown up in the water.
14. Recite the concluding verse uttered by the Lord.
As a lotus, fair and lovely, / By the water is not soiled, / By the world am I not soiled, / Therefore, Brahmin, am I a Buddha.
15. Are there many wonderful marvels which the monks delight to see in the Dhamma-Discipline?
Yes, there are. There are many wonderful marvels which the monks delight to see in the Dhamma-Discipline.
16. What simile did the Buddha use to describe the first marvel?
He used the simile of the mighty ocean that slopes down, slides and tends downward gradually without abrupt precipice.
17. What is the first marvel of the Dhamma-Discipline?
The first marvel is that the training is gradual, the progress is gradual, without abrupt penetration of knowledge.
18. What simile did the Buddha use to describe the second marvel?
He used the simile of the mighty ocean that is fixed and does not overpass its bounds.
19. What is the second marvel?
The second marvel is that the Buddhist disciples will not transgress the code of training even for life's sake.
20. What simile did the Buddha use to describe the third marvel?
He used the simile of the great rivers entering the mighty ocean, losing their former names and termed simply "ocean".
21. What is the third marvel?
The third marvel is that the four castes (the Khattiyas, the Brahmans, the Vessas and the Suddas) going forth from home to the homeless life in the Dhamma-Discipline, lose their former names and lineages and are regarded simply as recluses, sons of the Sakyas.
22. What simile did the Buddha use to describe the fourth marvel?
He used the simile of all the streams and the rains that affect neither the emptiness nor the fullness of the ocean.
23. What is the fourth marvel?
The fourth marvel is that neither the emptiness nor the fullness in the condition of Nibbana is seen though many monks pass finally away in that condition of Nibbana without remainder.
24. What simile did the Buddha use to describe the fifth marvel?
He used the simile of the mighty ocean that has only one taste, the taste of salt.
25. What is the fifth marvel?
The fifth marvel is that this Dhamma-Discipline has but one flavour, the flavour of liberation.
26. What simile did the Buddha use to describe the sixth marvel?
He used the simile of the ocean that has many and diverse gems: the pearl, the crystal and so forth.
27. What is the sixth marvel?
The sixth marvel is that the Dhamma-Discipline has many and diverse gems: the Four Foundations of Mindfulness, the Four Right Efforts, the Four Ways to Psychic Powers, the Five Faculties, the Five Powers, the Seven Limbs of Awakening, the Ariyan Eightfold Path.
28. What simile did the Buddha use to describe the seventh marvel?
He used the simile of the ocean that is the abode of great creatures: the Asuras, the Nagas, the Gandhabbas.
29. What is the seventh marvel?
The seventh marvel is that this Dhamma-Discipline is the abode of great beings: the four pairs of persons who make up the four kinds of holy disciples, who have attained the four paths and the four fruits (the Stream-Winner, the Once-Returner, the Non-Returner, the Arahant).
